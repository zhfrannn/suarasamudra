Tsunami Survivor Story

The Wave That Changed Everything

A 12-year-old's experience during the 2004 tsunami in Banda Aceh

Banda Aceh, December 26, 2004

Full Story Narration

"I was 12 years old when the water came. The sound still haunts me - like a thousand trains coming at once."

- Anonymous Survivor, Banda Aceh

It was a Sunday morning, December 26, 2004. I was playing with my younger brother in our yard when the ground started shaking. At first, we thought it was just another small earthquake - we were used to those in Aceh. But this one was different. It lasted much longer, and the shaking was so strong that we could barely stand.

My father came running out of the house, his face pale with worry. "Tsunami!" he shouted, using a word I had only heard in the old stories our grandmother told us. The "Smong" - the traditional warning our elders had passed down through generations. When the earth shakes and the sea retreats, you run to the hills.

We could see the ocean from our house, and what we saw next will be burned in my memory forever. The water was pulling back, back, back - further than I had ever seen. Fish were flopping on the exposed seabed, and some curious villagers were walking out to collect them. My father screamed at them to run, but they couldn't hear him from that distance.

Then came the sound. Like a freight train, like thunder, like the earth itself was crying out. A wall of dark water, taller than the coconut trees, was racing toward the shore. My father grabbed my hand and my mother scooped up my baby sister. "To the hills! Now!" he commanded.

We ran. We ran faster than I ever thought possible. Behind us, I could hear the water crashing, the sound of houses being torn apart, cars being tossed like toys. I didn't dare look back until we reached the mosque on the hill. When I finally turned around, our village was gone. Everything was underwater.

For three days, we stayed on that hill with hundreds of other survivors. We had nothing but the clothes on our backs. No food, no clean water, no shelter. But what I remember most wasn't the hunger or the fear - it was how people came together. Neighbors who had been feuding for years shared their last piece of bread. Former enemies became family.

The international aid came, and slowly we began to rebuild. But the real rebuilding wasn't just about houses and roads - it was about our hearts, our community, our understanding of what truly matters. The tsunami took everything we owned, but it also taught us that we are stronger together than apart.

Today, twenty years later, I work as a disaster preparedness educator. I share this story not to frighten people, but to honor those we lost and to ensure that the wisdom of our elders - the Smong - continues to save lives. When the earth shakes and the sea retreats, you run to the hills. This is our gift to future generations.
